# Anthropological Constraint Document
## Shroud of Turin — AI Forensic Reconstruction Project
### Version 1.0 — March 2026

---

## Purpose

This document defines the biological constraints for the AI facial reconstruction pipeline. These constraints prevent the AI from defaulting to Western art conventions and ensure the reconstruction reflects what archaeology, anthropology, and ancient DNA tell us about first-century Judean males.

CRITICAL PRINCIPLE: Where the Shroud's own depth data provides measurements (facial geometry, nose shape, jaw width, proportions), those measurements override population-level estimates. The Shroud records THIS SPECIFIC INDIVIDUAL. The anthropological constraints below are ONLY for characteristics the Shroud CANNOT provide — pigmentation, hair texture, eye color, and similar soft-tissue details not encoded in brightness-to-depth data.

## Source 1: Richard Neave Forensic Reconstruction (2001)

### Researcher
Richard Neave, retired medical artist, Unit of Art in Medicine, University of Manchester, England. Expert in forensic facial reconstruction with experience reconstructing Philip II of Macedon and Midas.

### Methodology
- Used first-century Jewish skulls obtained from a forensic science department in Israel
- CT scans created X-ray slices revealing minute structural data
- Software determined soft tissue thickness at multiple skull points
- Clay model built on cast skull with tissue depth markers
- Featured in BBC documentary "Son of God" (2001)

### Key Findings
- FACE SHAPE: Broad, wide face — significantly wider than Western art depictions
- NOSE: Large nose
- SKIN: Dark, olive-toned
- HAIR: Short, dark, curly (based on period artwork from archaeological sites and Paul's writing in 1 Corinthians 11:14 about long hair being a disgrace)
- BEARD: Short beard, consistent with Jewish tradition
- BUILD: Average Semite male of the era was approximately 155 cm (5'1") and 50 kg (110 lbs) based on skeletal remains

### Limitations
- Cartilage reconstruction (nose, ears) is inherently speculative from bone alone
- Debate exists about whether one or three skulls were used
- Skin/hair color derived from regional art, not direct evidence
- This is a population-representative face, NOT a specific individual

## Source 2: Joan Taylor — "What Did Jesus Look Like?" (2018)

### Researcher
Joan E. Taylor, Professor of Christian Origins and Second Temple Judaism, King's College London. Published by Bloomsbury T&T Clark, 262 pages.

### Methodology
- Consulted experts on ancient skeletons excavated in Israel
- Worked with Yossi Nagar, Israeli anthropologist
- Analyzed first-century artwork from Dura-Europos synagogue (3rd century, earliest known Jewish portrait art)
- Studied Ptolemaic-era papyri from Egypt describing Judean appearance
- Cross-referenced Gospel texts for clothing and grooming details

### Key Findings

#### Height
- Average first-century Judean male: 166 cm (5'5")
- Qumran skeletal data: male range 159-177 cm, low variation (CV=3.6, n=11)
- NOTE: The Shroud man is estimated at 175-185 cm — taller than average, but within the upper range of Qumran data

#### Skin
- Olive-brown skin tone
- Described as "a darker hue consistent with the skin tone of people of the Middle East"
- Supported by Ptolemaic-era papyri (2nd century BCE) describing Judeans as having "honey-colored skin"

#### Hair
- Dark-brown to black
- Short, likely curly or wavy
- Short beard

#### Eyes
- Deep brown / dark brown

#### Biological Affinity
- First-century Judeans were closest biologically to contemporary Iraqi Jews
- This finding comes from physical analysis of excavated skeletal remains
- Modern Ashkenazi, Sephardi, and other diaspora Jewish populations are NOT reliable proxies due to 2,000 years of admixture

#### Build and Lifestyle
- Jesus was described as a tektōn (artisan/carpenter), implying manual labor
- Outdoor lifestyle would produce weathered, tanned skin
- Gospels note nothing remarkable about his appearance, suggesting he was ordinary-looking for his context

### Taylor's Critique of Neave
- Taylor considers the BBC reconstruction "quite speculative" because cartilage reconstruction from bone is guesswork
- RELEVANCE TO OUR PROJECT: The Shroud provides soft tissue geometry (including nose shape) that skeletal reconstruction cannot. This is a significant advantage of our approach.

## Source 4: Ancient DNA and Genomic Studies

### Key Studies

#### Haber et al. 2017 (American Journal of Human Genetics)
- Sequenced five whole genomes from ~3,700-year-old Canaanite individuals from Sidon
- Found present-day Lebanese derive most ancestry from Canaanite-related populations
- Demonstrates substantial genetic continuity in the Levant since the Bronze Age
- Additional Eurasian ancestry arrived ~3,750-2,170 years ago during successive conquests

#### Agranat-Tamir et al. 2020 (Cell)
- Genome-wide DNA from 73 individuals across Bronze and Iron Age Southern Levant
- Populations modeled as mixture of local Neolithic populations + northeastern Near East populations
- Present-day Jewish groups and Levantine Arabic-speaking groups have 50%+ ancestry from Bronze Age Levantine-related populations
- Confirms post-Bronze-Age migrations significantly altered the region's genetics

#### Haber et al. 2013 (PLOS Genetics)
- Analyzed 500,000+ genome-wide SNPs in 1,341 Levantine samples
- Found recent genetic stratification driven by religious affiliation
- Pre-Islamic Levant was more genetically similar to Europeans than to broader Middle East
- Cultural changes in last 2,000 years drove admixture between geographically remote but culturally similar groups
- Confirms: modern populations DO NOT cleanly represent ancient ones

### Implications for Pigmentation
- Ancient Levantine populations had genetic profiles consistent with medium to dark olive skin
- No evidence for very light (northern European) or very dark (sub-Saharan) skin tones
- Hair and eye color genes consistent with dark brown to black hair, brown eyes
- Pigmentation inference from ancient DNA (using HIrisPlex-S system) supports olive-brown range for Levantine populations of this era

### Why Modern Populations Are Unreliable Proxies
- Ashkenazi Jews: ~41% European admixture (Agranat-Tamir 2020)
- Moroccan Jews: ~31% European admixture
- Modern Palestinians: admixture with Arabian Peninsula populations post-Islamic expansion
- Modern Lebanese: Eurasian ancestry not present in Bronze Age Levantines
- Samaritans: most genetically isolated and therefore closest to ancient Judean profile, but still 2,000 years of drift
