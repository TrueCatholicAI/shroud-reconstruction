Dorsal Body Analysis

Back-of-Body Depth Extraction and Candidate Surface Marking Detection

Overview

This analysis extends our forensic reconstruction to the dorsal (back) view of the body image on the Shroud of Turin. While the frontal body image has received extensive study, the dorsal surface provides additional evidence for systematic wound patterns that may correlate with crucifixion and scourging trauma.

Limitations

This analysis should be evaluated with awareness of the following limitations:

  • Over-Detection Concern: As noted in our frontal scourge analysis, the residual-based detection method may be susceptible to over-detection. The 115 candidate regions detected represent regions that exceed the residual threshold (54.86), but this threshold is based on image statistics rather than histological or anatomical criteria. Some detected regions may represent natural body surface variations, image artifacts, or textile weave impressions rather than actual wound marks.
  • Similarity Does Not Equal Accuracy: While the detected count (115) is numerically close to the literature estimate (~120), this numerical proximity should not be interpreted as validation of either figure. Two numbers can agree while both being systematically wrong due to shared methodological biases.
  • Parameter Dependence: The detection results are sensitive to the chosen threshold values. The residual threshold of 54.86 was derived from the processed image statistics, not from independent ground truth data about actual scourge marks.
  • No Histological Validation: The analysis operates purely on image-derived features without access to physical samples or forensic examination of the actual cloth.
  • Image Quality Considerations: The original photographs have been processed through multiple steps (photographic, digital conversion, alignment), potentially introducing artifacts that may be falsely interpreted as body markings.
  • Region Size Variability: The detected regions range from 10 to 1520 pixels with an average of 76.25 pixels. This high variability suggests that not all detected features represent similar types or scales of markings.

Conclusions

The dorsal body analysis detected 115 candidate mark regions using automated residual analysis. This number is numerically similar to published literature estimates of approximately 120 scourge marks. However, we cannot conclude from this analysis alone that either figure accurately represents the true number of scourge marks on the dorsal surface.

The detection methodology employed here suffers from the same over-detection concerns identified in our frontal scourge analysis. Without ground truth validation against known scourging injuries or independent expert assessment, we cannot distinguish between genuine wound marks and image features that share similar statistical properties.

Future work should focus on cross-validating these detections with independent forensic methods and conducting systematic false positive rate analysis to better understand the reliability of automated surface marking detection on Shroud images.
