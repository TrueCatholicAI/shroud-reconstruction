# Shroud of Turin AI Reconstruction - Video Script

**Format:** YouTube Shorts / TikTok (~3 minutes)
**Tone:** Measured, factual, visually driven. Not sensational. Let the images do the work.
**Music suggestion:** Low ambient/cinematic pad, no lyrics.

---

## HOOK (0:00 - 0:12)

**[VISUAL: Close-up of the Shroud face, slow zoom]**

> In 1976, scientists put a photograph of the Shroud of Turin into a machine that converts brightness to height.

**[VISUAL: Cut to VP-8 3D surface emerging from the image - the classic moment]**

> Every photograph they'd ever tested produced garbage. The Shroud produced a perfect three-dimensional face.

**[VISUAL: Hold on the 3D surface, rotating slowly]**

> Fifty years later, we took that discovery further.

---

## THE PIPELINE (0:12 - 0:45)

**[VISUAL: Side-by-side Enrie 1931 source photo → depth heatmap extraction]**

> We built an open-source computer vision pipeline that extracts the depth information encoded in the Shroud's image.

**[VISUAL: Depth map → 3D surface plot, angled view]**

> The brightness of every point on the Shroud corresponds to how far the cloth was from the body. Brighter means closer. We convert that into a three-dimensional surface.

**[VISUAL: Landmark overlay on depth map - eyes, nose, mouth marked]**

> Then we detect anatomical landmarks - eyes, nose, mouth, jawline - and measure the face in real centimeters.

**[VISUAL: Measurements table, numbers appearing one by one]**

> Nine measurements. Seven out of nine fall in normal adult male ranges. Both eyes, nose width, face width, jaw width - all anatomically correct.

---

## THE AI TEST (0:45 - 1:20)

**[VISUAL: MiDaS depth output vs VP-8 depth, side by side]**

> Here's where it gets interesting. We ran a state-of-the-art AI depth estimator on the same image. MiDaS - trained on ten million photographs to understand depth from shading and perspective.

**[VISUAL: Correlation number appearing: r = -0.07]**

> Correlation with the VP-8 depth: negative zero point zero seven. Essentially random noise.

**[VISUAL: Depth Anything V2 output added as third panel]**

> A second AI model - Depth Anything V2, trained on sixty-two million images, completely different architecture - also failed.

**[VISUAL: Correlation bar chart showing all near-zero values]**

> Neither model can see what the VP-8 sees. The three-dimensional information in the Shroud is not a standard photographic depth cue. It's something else entirely.

---

## CROSS-VALIDATION (1:20 - 1:50)

**[VISUAL: Enrie 1931 photo on left, Miller 1978 on right, arrow between]**

> To make sure this wasn't an artifact of one photograph, we repeated everything with a completely independent source - Vernon Miller's 1978 STURP photograph.

**[VISUAL: Side-by-side depth maps from both sources]**

> Different camera. Different film. Taken forty-seven years later. Same results. Seven of nine measurements in range. The signal is in the cloth, not the camera.

**[VISUAL: Formation analysis chart - linear R² = 1.000]**

> And when we plot intensity against distance, the relationship is perfectly linear. R-squared: one point zero zero zero zero. Not exponential. Not inverse square. Perfectly linear.

---

## THE RECONSTRUCTION (1:50 - 2:30)

**[VISUAL: Depth map morphing into clay reconstruction - slow, dramatic]**

> Using the extracted depth as a constraint, we generated sculptural reconstructions. The AI can only produce faces that match the geometry the Shroud encodes.

**[VISUAL: Clay → sandstone → marble renders, slow crossfade between each]**

> Clay. Sandstone. Marble. No skin color - because the Shroud doesn't encode color. That would be an assumption, not a finding.

**[VISUAL: Healed face comparison - original vs symmetrized side by side]**

> We also created a healed version by symmetrizing the depth map - removing the trauma asymmetry to see the underlying facial structure.

**[VISUAL: Full-body depth profile with height annotation]**

> The full-body analysis estimates a height of five foot nine. Hands crossed right over left. Thumbs not visible - consistent with post-mortem muscle contraction.
